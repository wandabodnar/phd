DNA structure and function

Environmental DNA (eDNA) analysis depends on one remarkable property of DNA: although it begins to degrade once released into the environment, fragments often remain sufficiently intact to be detected, amplified and sequenced. This persistence is possible because DNA is a chemically stable molecule capable of storing biological information over long periods of time.

To understand why eDNA methods work, it is necessary to understand how DNA is built, how it stores genetic information, and how its molecular structure influences stability, replication and detection. These same structural features determine where PCR primers bind, why short DNA fragments can be successfully amplified, and how DNA sequences can be used to distinguish one species from another.

DNA as a polymer

DNA (deoxyribonucleic acid) is a long biological polymer composed of repeating units called nucleotides. Each nucleotide contributes one “letter” to the DNA sequence, and the precise order of these nucleotides stores the hereditary information of an organism.

Unlike proteins, which are built from twenty different amino acids, DNA is constructed from only four types of nucleotides. Despite this apparent simplicity, the enormous number of possible nucleotide combinations enables DNA to encode the genetic information required for the development, function and reproduction of every known organism.

The structure of a nucleotide

Each nucleotide consists of three components:

  • a five-carbon sugar (deoxyribose);

  • a phosphate group;

  • a nitrogen-containing base.

The sugar and phosphate form the structural framework of DNA, while the nitrogenous bases carry the genetic information.

The four bases found in DNA are:

  • Adenine (A)

  • Thymine (T)

  • Cytosine (C)

  • Guanine (G)

The order, or sequence, of these bases stores the genetic information of an organism.

Building a DNA strand

Individual nucleotides are joined together by phosphodiester bonds. These covalent bonds link the phosphate group attached to the 5′ carbon of one nucleotide to the hydroxyl group attached to the 3′ carbon of the next nucleotide. As nucleotides are added, they form a continuous sugar–phosphate backbone. Importantly, the backbone is chemically uniform. The only part of the molecule that varies is the sequence of nitrogenous bases, which projects inward from the backbone. This distinction explains why DNA can maintain a stable overall structure while simultaneously storing enormous amounts of genetic information.

The double helix

DNA usually exists as two complementary strands twisted around one another to form the familiar double helix. The sugar–phosphate backbones lie on the outside of the molecule, while the nitrogenous bases project inward. Bases pair in a highly specific manner:

  • Adenine pairs with thymine through two hydrogen bonds.

  • Guanine pairs with cytosine through three hydrogen bonds.

These complementary base pairs hold the two strands together while maintaining the constant diameter of the double helix. Because each strand determines the sequence of its partner, either strand can serve as a template for DNA replication.

Antiparallel orientation

The two DNA strands do not run in the same direction. Instead, they are antiparallel. One strand runs from the 5′ end to the 3′ end, while the complementary strand runs from 3′ to 5′. This orientation is determined by the numbering of carbon atoms within the deoxyribose sugar. Although this arrangement may appear to be a structural detail, it has major biological consequences. DNA polymerases can synthesise DNA only by adding nucleotides to the free 3′ hydroxyl group of a growing strand. Consequently, every process involving DNA replication, repair and PCR depends on strand directionality.

DNA sequence: the language of biology

The order of nucleotides within DNA forms the biological equivalent of a written language. Just as changing a letter alters the meaning of a word, changing a nucleotide can alter genetic information.

For example:

ATGCTTAC…

and

ATGCGTAC…

differ by only a single nucleotide.

Although the difference appears small, it may distinguish one species from another or alter the function of a gene. Environmental DNA analysis exploits these sequence differences to identify organisms from tiny DNA fragments recovered from environmental samples.

The genetic code

The sequence of nucleotides in DNA contains the instructions needed to build proteins. However, proteins are composed of amino acids rather than nucleotides, so this genetic information must first be translated into a different molecular language.

The first step is transcription, in which the DNA sequence is copied into messenger RNA (mRNA). The mRNA molecule carries the genetic instructions from the nucleus to ribosomes, where proteins are synthesised. During translation, the mRNA sequence is read according to a set of rules known as the genetic code.

The genetic code is read in groups of three nucleotides, called codons. Each codon specifies either one amino acid or a signal to begin or end protein synthesis. For example, the codon ATG (or AUG in mRNA) encodes the amino acid methionine and usually serves as the start codon, whereas TAA, TAG and TGA function as stop codons.

Because each codon contains three nucleotide positions and each position can contain one of four bases, there are 4 × 4 × 4 = 64 possible codons. Of these, 61 encode the 20 standard amino acids, while three function as stop signals. Since several codons specify the same amino acid, the genetic code is described as degenerate, or redundant.

Although the genetic code explains how DNA directs protein synthesis, most eDNA studies do not analyse proteins. Instead, they examine the DNA sequence itself. As DNA sequences accumulate mutations over evolutionary time, they become increasingly different among species, providing the sequence variation required for species identification.

Conserved and variable regions

Not all DNA evolves at the same rate. Some regions perform essential biological functions and therefore change very little during evolution. These are known as conserved regions. Other regions accumulate mutations more rapidly and differ substantially among species. These are referred to as variable regions. This combination of conservation and variation makes molecular identification possible. Conserved regions provide stable binding sites for PCR primers. The variable sequence located between these primer binding sites contains the information required to distinguish species. This principle underlies nearly every eDNA metabarcoding assay.

From DNA structure to environmental DNA

The molecular structure of DNA underpins every stage of environmental DNA analysis. After DNA is released into the environment, its chemical stability allows some fragments to survive despite continuous degradation. During PCR, the DNA strands are separated by heating, allowing primers to bind to conserved target regions. DNA polymerase then amplifies the intervening variable region, producing millions of copies for sequencing. The resulting sequences are compared with reference databases to identify the organisms that contributed the environmental DNA.

Connecting the concepts

Nucleotides are linked together by phosphodiester bonds to form DNA strands, and two complementary strands pair to create the DNA double helix. The sequence of nucleotides stores genetic information, which is interpreted according to the genetic code to produce proteins. Over evolutionary time, mutations generate conserved and variable regions within DNA. Conserved regions provide binding sites for PCR primers, while the intervening variable regions are amplified and sequenced to enable species identification.

Key concepts

At the end of this chapter, the most important ideas are:

  • DNA is composed of repeating nucleotide units.

  • Nucleotides are linked by phosphodiester bonds to form a sugar–phosphate backbone.

  • Two complementary DNA strands form the double helix.

  • DNA strands are antiparallel.

  • Genetic information is stored in the sequence of nucleotides.

  • The genetic code translates nucleotide triplets (codons) into amino acids during protein synthesis.

  • Conserved regions provide primer-binding sites, while variable regions enable species identification in eDNA analyses.
