Genetic markers and primer design

In the previous chapter, we learned that mitochondrial DNA is widely used in environmental DNA (eDNA) studies because its high copy number increases the probability of detecting degraded DNA fragments. However, sequencing an entire mitochondrial genome from every organism present in an environmental sample is neither practical nor necessary.

Instead, eDNA studies target short regions of DNA known as genetic markers. These markers contain sufficient sequence variation to distinguish species while remaining conserved enough to allow universal amplification using PCR.

Selecting the appropriate genetic marker is one of the most important decisions in any eDNA study. Marker choice determines which organisms can be detected, the taxonomic resolution that can be achieved, and the overall reliability of species identification.

This chapter introduces the principles of genetic marker selection and explains how PCR primers are designed to amplify the most informative regions of DNA.

What is a genetic marker?

A genetic marker is a specific region of DNA used to identify organisms or distinguish between species. Rather than analysing an entire genome, researchers examine relatively short DNA sequences that contain informative genetic variation.

The ideal marker should satisfy two important criteria:

  • it should be highly conserved at its ends so that universal PCR primers can bind across many species;

  • it should contain a variable internal region that differs among species and provides taxonomic information.

These properties allow DNA from many organisms to be amplified simultaneously while retaining enough sequence variation for species identification.

DNA barcoding and metabarcoding

Traditional DNA barcoding identifies a single organism by sequencing one standard genetic marker.

In contrast, DNA metabarcoding analyses environmental samples that contain DNA from many species simultaneously. Universal primers amplify the same genetic marker from all organisms present in the sample, and high-throughput sequencing generates millions of DNA sequences that are assigned to individual taxa using reference databases.

While DNA barcoding focuses on individual specimens, metabarcoding provides a comprehensive assessment of entire biological communities.

Characteristics of an ideal genetic marker

No single genetic marker is suitable for every organism or research question. However, an effective marker generally possesses several important characteristics.

An ideal marker should:

  • be present in all target organisms

  • contain conserved primer-binding regions

  • include sufficient sequence variation to distinguish species

  • be short enough to amplify degraded environmental DNA

  • have comprehensive reference sequences available in public databases

Balancing these characteristics is often the greatest challenge in marker selection.

Common genetic markers

Different taxonomic groups require different genetic markers because evolutionary rates vary among genes and organisms.

12S rRNA

The mitochondrial 12S ribosomal RNA gene is one of the most widely used markers for vertebrate eDNA studies. Its conserved primer-binding regions and short variable regions make it particularly suitable for degraded environmental DNA.

Typical applications include fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds, and mammals.

Cytochrome c oxidase subunit I (COI)

COI is the standard DNA barcode for animals and provides excellent species-level resolution. However, the full-length barcode is approximately 650 base pairs long, making it less suitable for fragmented environmental DNA. Consequently, many eDNA studies employ shorter COI mini-barcodes.

16S rRNA

The 16S ribosomal RNA gene is widely used to study bacteria and archaea. Conserved regions permit universal amplification, while variable regions enable taxonomic classification.

18S rRNA

The 18S ribosomal RNA gene is highly conserved and is commonly used to investigate diverse eukaryotic communities, particularly protists and other microscopic organisms.

Primer design

PCR requires two short DNA molecules known as primers. A forward primer binds to one DNA strand. A reverse primer binds to the complementary strand. Together, they define the DNA region that will be amplified. Primers are typically 18–30 nucleotides long and are designed to bind to conserved regions that flank a variable DNA sequence. Because PCR can amplify only the DNA located between the primers, primer design determines which organisms are detected. Poorly designed primers may fail to amplify certain species, resulting in biased biodiversity estimates.

Universal and taxon-specific primers

Not all primers are designed for the same purpose. Universal primers amplify DNA from a wide range of organisms and are commonly used in biodiversity surveys. Taxon-specific primers target particular groups, such as fish, amphibians or mammals, while excluding unrelated organisms. The choice between universal and taxon-specific primers depends on the objectives of the study.

Marker selection in eDNA studies

Choosing an appropriate marker requires balancing several competing factors. Researchers must consider:

  • the target taxonomic group

  • expected DNA degradation

  • required taxonomic resolution

  • availability of reference sequences

  • sequencing platform

  • study objectives

No marker is universally superior. The most appropriate marker depends on the biological question being addressed.

From marker selection to PCR

Marker selection begins by defining the target organisms or taxonomic group to be studied. An appropriate genetic marker is then chosen based on the objectives of the study and the characteristics of the target DNA. PCR primers are designed to bind to conserved regions flanking the marker, allowing the intervening variable region to be amplified. The amplified DNA is subsequently sequenced, and the resulting sequences are compared with reference databases to identify the species present in the environmental sample.

Connecting the concepts

Genetic markers are short regions of DNA selected because they contain both conserved and variable sequences. Conserved regions provide reliable binding sites for PCR primers, while the intervening variable region contains the genetic differences required for species identification. Marker selection therefore determines which organisms can be detected and how accurately they can be identified.

Key concepts

At the end of this chapter, the most important ideas are:

  • Genetic markers are short DNA regions used for species identification.

  • An ideal marker combines conserved primer-binding sites with variable internal sequences.

  • Different taxonomic groups require different genetic markers.

  • PCR primers determine which DNA regions are amplified.

  • Marker choice directly influences detection success and taxonomic resolution.
