From living organisms to environmental DNA

In the previous chapter, we learned that DNA stores the genetic information required to build and maintain every living organism. However, environmental DNA analysis does not study DNA inside living cells. Instead, it relies on fragments of genetic material that have been released into the environment.

This immediately raises an important question.

How does DNA leave an organism and become environmental DNA?

Answering this question is essential because every stage of eDNA analysis depends on understanding where environmental DNA originates, how it changes after release, and why only a small fraction survives long enough to be detected.

What is environmental DNA?

Environmental DNA (eDNA) is genetic material recovered directly from environmental samples such as water, soil, sediment, snow, ice or air without first isolating the target organisms. Importantly, eDNA is not chemically different from DNA inside a living cell. The difference lies entirely in its location.

Inside an organism, DNA is protected by cellular membranes, organelles, proteins and repair systems. Once released into the environment, these protective mechanisms disappear, and DNA becomes exposed to physical, chemical and biological degradation.

Consequently, environmental DNA should be viewed as ordinary DNA existing under extraordinary conditions.

Sources of environmental DNA

Every organism continuously releases genetic material into its surroundings. This release occurs throughout life and continues after death.

Major sources include shed skin cells, mucus, faeces, urine, saliva, blood, scales, feathers and hair, reproductive cells, decomposing tissues and decaying carcasses.

Aquatic organisms constantly release cells into surrounding water, while terrestrial organisms contribute DNA to soil and to the atmosphere.

Environmental DNA therefore represents a mixture of genetic material originating from many individuals and species simultaneously.

Intracellular and extracellular DNA

Once released, environmental DNA exists in two principal forms.

Intracellular DNA

Some DNA remains enclosed within intact or partially intact cells, mitochondria or other cellular structures.

These membranes provide temporary protection from degradation, increasing the likelihood that DNA survives long enough for detection.

Extracellular DNA

Other DNA exists freely within the environment.

Extracellular DNA may:

  • dissolve in water;

  • adsorb onto clay minerals;

  • bind to organic particles;

  • become incorporated into sediments;

  • attach to biofilms.

Extracellular DNA is generally more vulnerable to enzymatic degradation but may persist surprisingly long when protected by mineral surfaces.

Environmental transport

DNA rarely remains where it was released. Water currents transport DNA downstream. Wind redistributes airborne particles. Sediments trap DNA and may later release it. Animals themselves may transport DNA on their bodies or within their digestive systems. Consequently, detecting DNA does not necessarily indicate that the organism was present at the precise sampling location. Instead, environmental DNA reflects a combination of biological activity, environmental transport and degradation. Understanding these processes is essential when interpreting survey results.

DNA degradation

Once outside living cells, DNA immediately begins to degrade. Several processes contribute simultaneously.

Enzymatic degradation

Microorganisms release nucleases that digest DNA into progressively smaller fragments.

Ultraviolet radiation

Sunlight damages DNA by producing lesions such as thymine dimers, reducing successful amplification.

Temperature

Higher temperatures accelerate both chemical reactions and microbial activity, increasing degradation rates.

pH

Highly acidic or alkaline environments promote hydrolysis of chemical bonds within DNA.

Mechanical processes

Water movement, sediment abrasion and repeated freeze–thaw cycles physically fragment DNA molecules.

These mechanisms rarely operate independently. Instead, environmental DNA usually experiences several degradation processes simultaneously.

Why environmental DNA ss fragmented

Unlike genomic DNA extracted directly from fresh tissue, environmental DNA is rarely intact. Instead, it consists primarily of short fragments. In aquatic environments, fragments shorter than approximately 200 base pairs are particularly common. This fragmentation has profound consequences for molecular analysis.

Long DNA targets are unlikely to remain intact. Short targets survive much more frequently. This simple observation explains why most environmental DNA studies amplify fragments between approximately 80 and 150 base pairs.

Marker choice is therefore driven not only by evolutionary biology but also by molecular degradation.

Persistence

Environmental DNA does not disappear immediately after an organism leaves an area. Persistence depends on environmental conditions.

In flowing rivers, detectable DNA may remain for only hours or days. In lakes, persistence may extend to several weeks. Within sediments, protected DNA may survive for months or even years.

Persistence increases the probability of detection but also introduces uncertainty. DNA may indicate recent rather than current presence. Consequently, eDNA surveys must always be interpreted within their environmental context.

The journey of environmental DNA

Living organisms shed cells and tissues, releasing DNA into the environment, where it is transported by water, wind, or sediments before undergoing fragmentation and degradation; this environmental DNA is then sampled, extracted, amplified by PCR, sequenced, and used for species identification.

Key concepts

  • Environmental DNA is ordinary DNA found outside living organisms.

  • DNA originates from many biological sources.

  • Environmental DNA exists in both intracellular and extracellular forms.

  • DNA is transported through the environment before sampling.

  • Physical, chemical and biological processes fragment DNA continuously.

  • Environmental degradation strongly influences marker selection and laboratory methods.
