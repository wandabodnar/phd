eDNA timeline overview

1980s: Foundations of DNA fingerprinting and environmental DNA

  • DNA fingerprinting established (Jeffreys et al. 1985)

    • Discovery of hypervariable minisatellite regions.

    • First individual-specific DNA “barcode.”

    • Laid the foundation for forensic genetics, paternity testing, and later molecular ecology.

  • The term “environmental DNA” introduced (Ogram et al. 1987)

    • Developed methods to extract intracellular and extracellular DNA directly from sediments.

    • Introduced the concept of recovering DNA directly from environmental samples without isolating organisms.

  • PCR enables DNA fingerprinting from tiny samples

    • Jeffreys et al. (1988): PCR amplification of minisatellites made DNA fingerprinting practical from very small samples.

    • Hoelzel et al. (1988): DNA fingerprinting applied to whale skin biopsies, demonstrating non-lethal genetic monitoring of wildlife.

Major breakthrough of the decade: DNA became a practical tool for identifying individuals, and environmental DNA was first recognised as a recoverable genetic resource.

1990s: From DNA fingerprinting to DNA barcodes and early environmental DNA applications

  • Giovannoni et al. 1990 analysed 16S ribosomal RNA (rRNA) gene sequences directly from a microbial community in the Sargasso Sea, providing one of the first culture-independent surveys of environmental microbial diversity.

  • Amos et al. 1992 demonstrated that DNA recovered from naturally shed material, such as whale skin, could be used for genetic analysis.

  • Arnot et al. 1993 Introduced the concept of using distinctive genetic sequence patterns as biological identifiers, and the term “DNA barcode” was first used to describe molecular signatures capable of distinguishing biological samples.

  • Paget et al. 1998 demonstrated that plant DNA could be recovered directly from soil, providing early evidence that environmental samples preserve genetic traces of larger organisms.

Major breakthrough of the decade: DNA analysis expanded from identifying individual organisms to characterising entire biological communities. Environmental sequencing revealed hidden microbial diversity, DNA barcoding introduced genetic identification frameworks, and early macrobial eDNA studies demonstrated that environmental samples could act as archives of biodiversity. Together, these advances established the foundations of modern eDNA metabarcoding, biodiversity assessment, and molecular ecology.

2000s: Environmental DNA becomes a tool for biodiversity reconstruction, pollution tracking, and species detection

DNA persistence and the ecological role of dissolved DNA (Matsui et al. 2001)

  • Demonstrated that extracellular dissolved DNA (dDNA) can persist in aquatic environments, with DNA fragments of approximately 400 bp surviving for up to 7 days at 18°C.

  • Provided early experimental evidence that environmental DNA is not immediately degraded after release into water, supporting its use as a molecular signal for species detection.

  • Highlighted the natural occurrence of dissolved extracellular DNA, which enters ecosystems through cell death, shedding of tissues, and biological secretions.

  • Showed that environmental DNA persistence is influenced by environmental factors including UV radiation, temperature, and nuclease activity.

  • Established that dissolved DNA is not only genetic information but also an important ecological resource, providing microorganisms with carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus.

  • Demonstrated that microbes can utilise extracellular DNA through enzymatic degradation, recycling genetic material as nutrients.

UV radiation identified as a major factor affecting DNA persistence (Ravanet et al. 2001)

  • Highlighted UV exposure as an important abiotic factor influencing the persistence and detectability of environmental DNA.

  • Contributed to understanding why eDNA signals vary between habitats and why DNA preservation differs among ecosystems.

First metagenetic analysis of soil eDNA (Willerslev et al. 2003)

  • Demonstrated that DNA extracted from permafrost and sediment samples could reveal past biodiversity in the absence of visible fossils or macrofossils.

  • Recovered ancient DNA sequences from Siberian permafrost cores dating from 400,000 to 10,000 years ago, including plant taxa and megafaunal species such as mammoth, bison, and horse.

  • Showed that sedimentary DNA could reconstruct historical changes in ecosystem composition and biodiversity through time.

  • Established environmental DNA as a powerful archive for studying past ecosystems and long-term ecological change.

Environmental DNA as a resource for microorganisms (Chen and Dubnau 2004)

  • Expanded understanding of the ecological functions of extracellular DNA by showing that bacteria can actively acquire environmental DNA.

  • Demonstrated two major roles of environmental DNA for microorganisms:

    • Genetic resource: DNA from related bacteria may provide templates for repairing damaged genetic material.

    • Nutritional resource: DNA can be degraded and used as a source of carbon, nitrogen, and phosphorus.

  • Highlighted that extracellular DNA is an active component of microbial ecosystems rather than simply degraded biological material.

First microbial and faecal eDNA detection in river water (Martellini et al. 2005)

  • Developed a PCR-based method to detect and distinguish faecal contamination from human, bovine, ovine, and porcine sources in aquatic environments.

  • Used species-specific mitochondrial DNA markers to identify the origin of pollution in wastewater, agricultural runoff, and river samples.

  • Represented the first application of eukaryotic genetic material from water samples to trace animal-derived pollution sources.

  • Demonstrated the potential of eDNA approaches for environmental monitoring, water quality assessment, and microbial source tracking.

eDNA enables detection of invasive and cryptic species (Ficetola et al. 2008)

  • Developed one of the first widely recognised applications of eDNA for detecting a target species in freshwater ecosystems.

  • Used species-specific mitochondrial DNA primers to detect American bullfrog DNA in controlled experiments and natural wetlands.

  • Successfully identified populations even when species densities were low and traditional surveys were ineffective.

  • Demonstrated that eDNA could provide a sensitive, non-invasive method for monitoring invasive, rare, and difficult-to-detect species.

  • Highlighted challenges associated with interpreting eDNA signals, including uncertainty over how long DNA remains detectable after an organism leaves an environment.

DNA barcoding becomes integrated into ecology (Valentini et al. 2008)

  • Provided an overview of DNA barcoding as a method for species identification using standardised genetic markers.

  • Demonstrated the value of molecular identification tools for ecological studies, biodiversity surveys, and species monitoring.

  • Helped connect traditional ecological research with emerging molecular approaches.

Non-invasive genetics advances ecological monitoring (Beja-Pereira et al. 2009)

  • Reviewed the development of non-invasive genetic sampling approaches for wildlife studies.

  • Showed how DNA obtained from naturally shed biological material could transform population monitoring and conservation genetics.

  • Highlighted technological improvements that increased the feasibility of studying wildlife without capture or disturbance.

  • Provided important methodological foundations for later vertebrate eDNA applications.

Major breakthrough of the decade: Environmental DNA transitioned from a concept describing DNA persistence in the environment into a practical ecological monitoring tool. During the 2000s, researchers demonstrated that DNA could be recovered from sediments, water, and naturally shed material to reconstruct past ecosystems, trace pollution sources, and detect rare or invasive species. Advances in PCR, DNA barcoding, and non-invasive genetics transformed eDNA from a theoretical possibility into a powerful method for biodiversity assessment and conservation biology.
