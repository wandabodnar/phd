Mitochondrial DNA

In the previous chapter, we learned that DNA stores genetic information in the sequence of nucleotide bases and that differences in these sequences allow species to be distinguished. However, environmental samples contain only tiny amounts of highly fragmented DNA originating from many different organisms. This raises an important question:

If every cell contains thousands of genes, why do most eDNA studies analyse only a few specific regions of mitochondrial DNA?

The answer lies in the unique biological properties of mitochondria. Compared with nuclear DNA, mitochondrial DNA is present in many more copies per cell, has a compact genome, and contains genes that combine highly conserved regions with species-specific variation. These characteristics make mitochondrial DNA particularly well suited for detecting organisms from trace amounts of degraded environmental DNA.

What are mitochondria?

Mitochondria are membrane-bound organelles found in almost all eukaryotic cells. Their primary role is to produce adenosine triphosphate (ATP), the universal energy currency of the cell, through cellular respiration.

Unlike most organelles, mitochondria possess their own genome. This is because they evolved from free-living bacteria that entered into a symbiotic relationship with ancestral eukaryotic cells more than 1.5 billion years ago. Although most of the original bacterial genes have been transferred to the nucleus during evolution, mitochondria have retained a small circular genome that is replicated independently of nuclear DNA.

This evolutionary history explains why mitochondria contain their own DNA and why mitochondrial genes differ from those found in the nucleus.

The mitochondrial genome

The mitochondrial genome is much smaller than the nuclear genome. For example, the human nuclear genome contains approximately 3.2 billion base pairs, whereas the mitochondrial genome consists of only about 16,500 base pairs. Despite its small size, mitochondrial DNA contains genes that are essential for cellular respiration, including protein-coding genes, transfer RNA (tRNA) genes and ribosomal RNA (rRNA) genes.

Unlike nuclear chromosomes, mitochondrial DNA is:

  • circular rather than linear

  • present in many copies per cell

  • inherited almost exclusively from the mother in most animals

  • not packaged into chromosomes

Although compact, the mitochondrial genome contains sufficient sequence variation to distinguish many species.

Why mitochondrial DNA is ideal for eDNA

Environmental DNA studies are essentially a search for extremely rare DNA fragments. The probability of detecting DNA depends largely on how many copies of the target sequence are present in the original sample. Each eukaryotic cell contains only two copies of most nuclear genes but may contain hundreds to thousands of copies of mitochondrial DNA because every cell contains numerous mitochondria, each carrying multiple copies of its genome. This dramatically increases the probability that at least one copy of a mitochondrial gene survives environmental degradation and is recovered during sampling.

The large number of mitochondrial genomes present in each cell means that many copies of the target DNA are released into the environment. Although most fragments eventually degrade, the high initial copy number increases the likelihood that some remain intact long enough to be sampled, amplified and sequenced, thereby improving detection sensitivity.

For this reason, mitochondrial DNA is generally much easier to detect than nuclear DNA in environmental samples.

Mitochondrial genes used in eDNA

Not every mitochondrial gene is equally useful. An ideal genetic marker should satisfy two requirements:

  1. Conserved regions that allow universal PCR primers to bind across many species.

  2. Variable regions that differ sufficiently to distinguish species.

Several mitochondrial genes meet these criteria.

12S ribosomal RNA (12S rRNA)

The 12S rRNA gene is one of the most widely used markers for vertebrate eDNA studies. Its short variable regions are flanked by highly conserved sequences, allowing universal primers to amplify DNA from a wide range of fish, amphibians, reptiles, birds and mammals.

Cytochrome c oxidase subunit I (COI)

COI is the standard DNA barcode for animals and provides excellent species resolution. However, the full-length barcode is relatively long and often unsuitable for degraded environmental DNA unless shorter “mini-barcode” regions are used.

Cytochrome b (Cyt b)

Cytochrome b is frequently used in phylogenetic studies and species identification but is less commonly employed in high-throughput metabarcoding because it generally requires longer DNA fragments.

The choice of marker therefore depends on the organisms being studied, the condition of the DNA and the objectives of the investigation.

Mitochondrial DNA is not perfect

Although mitochondrial DNA offers many advantages, it also has limitations. Because it is inherited maternally in most animals, mitochondrial DNA represents only a single genetic lineage. It therefore provides less information about population genetics than nuclear DNA. Some closely related species possess nearly identical mitochondrial sequences, making reliable discrimination difficult. In addition, the number of mitochondrial genomes varies among tissues and species, meaning that mitochondrial DNA abundance does not necessarily reflect organism abundance. Consequently, eDNA studies detect DNA, not individuals.

From mitochondrial DNA to species identification

Living cells contain numerous mitochondria, and each mitochondrion carries multiple copies of mitochondrial DNA. When cells are shed or die, these mitochondrial genomes are released into the environment. Although many DNA molecules degrade over time, some fragments remain sufficiently intact to be recovered during sampling. In the laboratory, PCR amplifies short mitochondrial DNA markers, which are then sequenced. The resulting sequences are compared with reference databases to identify the species from which the environmental DNA originated.

Key concepts

At the end of this chapter, the most important ideas are:

  • Mitochondria possess their own independent genome.

  • Mitochondrial DNA is present in many more copies than nuclear DNA.

  • High copy number increases the probability of detecting degraded environmental DNA.

  • Mitochondrial genes contain both conserved and variable regions, making them excellent genetic markers.

  • Mitochondrial DNA is highly effective for species detection but has important biological limitations.
