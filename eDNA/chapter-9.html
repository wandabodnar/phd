Interpreting environmental DNA data

After bioinformatic analysis, researchers obtain a list of species detected in each environmental sample. Although this information appears straightforward, interpreting eDNA results requires careful consideration of both biological and technical factors.

The detection of environmental DNA confirms that DNA from a species was present in the sampled environment, but it does not necessarily indicate the number of individuals, their precise location, or whether they are currently alive. DNA can be transported, diluted and degraded before sampling, and every stage of the eDNA workflow introduces uncertainty.

Understanding what eDNA can and cannot tell us is essential for drawing reliable ecological conclusions and making informed management decisions.

What does eDNA detect?

Environmental DNA analysis detects DNA molecules, not organisms themselves. When a species is identified from an environmental sample, the result indicates that DNA from that species was present in the sampled environment. The DNA may have originated from living individuals, recently deceased organisms, shed cells, faeces, mucus, gametes or other biological material. Consequently, eDNA provides evidence of species presence but does not directly observe the organism.

Presence and absence

The simplest interpretation of eDNA data is whether a species has been detected. A positive detection indicates that DNA matching a particular species was recovered and identified. However, the absence of a detection does not necessarily mean that the species is absent. DNA concentrations may be extremely low, environmental conditions may accelerate DNA degradation, or laboratory and analytical processes may fail to recover or amplify the target DNA. For this reason, eDNA studies often use repeated sampling and statistical methods to estimate detection probability.

Can eDNA estimate abundance?

One of the most common questions in eDNA research is whether the amount of DNA reflects the abundance of a species. In some situations, higher eDNA concentrations are associated with greater numbers or biomass of organisms. However, this relationship is influenced by many additional factors, including:

  • rates of DNA shedding

  • organism size

  • water temperature

  • microbial activity

  • water movement

  • DNA degradation

As a result, eDNA concentration should generally be interpreted as an indicator of relative rather than absolute abundance.

False positives and false negatives

No monitoring method is completely free from error. A false positive occurs when a species is reported even though it is not truly present. Possible causes include contamination, sequencing errors or incorrect taxonomic assignment. A false negative occurs when a species is present but is not detected. This may result from low DNA concentration, inefficient sampling, DNA degradation, PCR inhibition or insufficient sequencing depth. Understanding these errors is essential for interpreting eDNA results with confidence.

Factors influencing detection

Species detection depends on many interacting biological and environmental factors. These include:

  • organism abundance

  • DNA shedding rates

  • environmental conditions

  • transport of DNA

  • degradation rates

  • sampling strategy

  • laboratory procedures

  • sequencing depth

  • bioinformatic analysis

Because each stage influences the final result, careful study design is essential for obtaining reliable biodiversity data.

Detection probability

Environmental DNA surveys rarely detect every species present. Instead, each species has a probability of detection, representing the likelihood that its DNA will be recovered and identified if the species is present. Detection probability generally increases with:

  • larger sample volumes

  • multiple field replicates

  • efficient DNA extraction

  • successful PCR amplification

  • greater sequencing depth

Estimating detection probability helps researchers account for imperfect detection when analysing biodiversity.

From species lists to ecological information

Once species have been identified, the data can be used to answer ecological questions. Researchers may:

  • compare biodiversity among sites

  • monitor changes over time

  • detect invasive species

  • identify threatened species

  • assess ecosystem restoration

  • evaluate conservation management

Thus, eDNA provides a powerful tool for understanding biological communities without directly observing every organism.

Connecting the concepts

Environmental DNA analysis produces species detections rather than direct observations of organisms. Interpreting these detections requires an understanding of DNA persistence, transport, laboratory methods and statistical uncertainty. When these factors are considered, eDNA provides valuable information for monitoring biodiversity and supporting ecological research.

Key concepts

At the end of this chapter, the most important ideas are:

  • eDNA detects DNA rather than organisms.

  • Positive detections indicate the presence of species DNA in the sampled environment.

  • Negative results do not always indicate species absence.

  • Detection probability varies among species and environments.

  • False positives and false negatives are important sources of uncertainty.

  • eDNA data support a wide range of ecological and conservation applications.
