üíß Water collection

Goal: Capture a snapshot of the aquatic community by collecting DNA shed by organisms into the water.

  • Collect surface water using bottles or a bucket.

    • Use sterile sampling bottles and gloves to avoid contamination.

  • Optional: record environmental variables (e.g. salinity, temperature, DO).

üß™ Filtration

Goal: Concentrate the trace DNA onto a membrane.

  • Filter the water through a filter (e.g. Waterra‚Ñ¢) using a peristaltic pump or syringe.

    • These filters trap environmental DNA (eDNA) and cell fragments on the internal membrane.

  • Store filters at -20‚ÄØ¬∞C or lower to preserve DNA integrity.

üß¨ DNA extraction

Goal: Release and purify the DNA from the filters and remove unwanted material.

Resuspension of the eDNA

  1. Remove the Waterra filters from the freezer in advance and allow them to defrost in the fridge for approximately one hour before extraction.

  2. Clean work area with 25% bleach solution and blue roll.

  3. Fill two 50‚ÄØmL Falcon tubes (per Waterra) with TE resuspension buffer, making sure to label the tubes with the sample number.

  4. Shake out any residual water from the inlet of the filter. Apply parafilm to cover the smooth outlet of the filter.

  5. Pipette 50‚ÄØmL of the TE resuspension buffer into the capsule through the ridged inlet and seal with more parafilm.

  6. Agitate capsule for five minutes.

    • A fingertip can be placed over each end and the capsule manually shaken, or if parafilm is securely taped into place, the capsule can be held against a vortex.

  7. Pour the solution back into the first 50‚ÄØmL Falcon tube, and repeat steps 5 and 6 for the second 50‚ÄØmL Falcon tube, ending up with two 50‚ÄØmL Falcon tubes of eDNA resuspended from the filter in turbid TE buffer.

    • The spent Waterra filter can be thrown away.
What is TE Buffer?

TE buffer (Tris-EDTA, pH 8.0) is used to gently release DNA from inside the Waterra capsule.

  • Tris or Tris(hydroxymethyl) aminomethane: stabilises pH

    • Maintaining a stable, slightly alkaline pH is crucial to prevent DNA degradation caused by hydrolysis, which breaks the bonds between nucleotides.

    • If pH drops (becomes acidic), DNA is more prone to hydrolytic cleavage ‚Äî this breaks the bonds between nucleotides and can irreversibly damage the DNA, interfering with downstream steps like PCR and sequencing.

  • EDTA (Ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid): binds divalent cations (e.g. Mg¬≤‚Å∫, Ca¬≤‚Å∫) to inhibit DNases and protect DNA integrity

Centrifuging

  1. Centrifuge the two 50‚ÄØmL Falcon tubes for 10 minutes at 4500 rpm (revolutions per minute, a unit that measures the rotational speed of a centrifuge rotor) to separate debris/sediment from the liquid DNA-containing fraction.
    • Supernatant: the clear upper layer, containing eDNA in solution
    • Pellet: the sediment settled at the bottom of the tube

Filtering through funnel filter

  1. An analytical test filter funnel attached to a Buchner funnel and peristaltic pump is used to filter the buffer mix through a 0.45-micron disc filter (Nalgene‚Ñ¢).

  2. Combine the two Falcon tubes per sample by pouring the now clear supernatant from both tubes through the funnel, trying to retain the sediment in the tubes.

  3. Adjust the speed of the peristaltic pump to pull the supernatant through the funnel, with an additional minute of filtering to dry the funnel filter disc.

    • Samples which required multiple filters due to build-up of sediment can be consolidated into one analytical filter funnel.

  4. Upon completion, one funnel filter disc should contain the filtered supernatant, and the remaining sediment should be retained in the original two Falcon tubes.

Buffer & kit components

  • PW1 (lysis buffer): Breaks open cell walls and membranes

    • Lysis: the breakdown of a cell caused by damage to its plasma (outer) membrane

  • Proteinase K: Degrades proteins and DNases that might otherwise destroy DNA

    • Lipids and proteins can act as PCR inhibitors

  • Beads (inside the bead tube): Provide mechanical disruption during vortexing, especially for tough cell walls (e.g. bacteria, algae)

  • QIAshredder column (later step): Removes cellular debris, allowing only the clean lysate to pass through

    • Lysate is the fluid that results from breaking open cells ‚Äî a mixture that contains all the internal contents of the cells just lysed (broken apart).
    • In DNA extraction:
      • Start by disrupting the cells (e.g. with Proteinase K and lysis buffers).
      • This releases the DNA into the solution ‚Äî now called a lysate.
      • The goal is to clean up this lysate to isolate just the DNA, removing all the other cellular ‚Äújunk‚Äù.
Additional notes

  • All extractions were carried out in a lateral flow cabinet, regularly sterilised with 25% bleach (dH‚ÇÇO) to minimise contamination.

  • Bottles were autoclaved, and all other equipment was sterilised with 50% bleach prior to use.

  • An additional chemical lysis step was incorporated by adding 100‚ÄØ¬µL Proteinase K and 900‚ÄØ¬µL PW1 to Eppendorf tubes containing dry filter paper.

  • Samples were incubated in a heat block at 56‚ÄØ¬∞C overnight with agitation to promote thorough cell lysis.

  • During lysis, Waterra disc filters absorbed a large volume of lysate. To recover DNA:

    • The soaked filter fragments were transferred into a QIAshredder column
    • The remaining lysate was pooled before proceeding with the DNeasy protocol

  • A negative control was included in every extraction batch.

  • All extractions and controls were quantified using a Qubit v2 fluorometer with the dsDNA BR Assay Kit (Invitrogen).

üî¨ DNA quantification (Qubit)

Goal: Measures how much double-stranded DNA (dsDNA) there is in the extract.

  • Prepare Qubit working solution:

    • Mix dye and buffer at a 1:200 ratio (e.g. 199‚ÄØ¬µL buffer + 1‚ÄØ¬µL dye per sample).

  • Label assay tubes for each standard and sample.

  • Add 190‚ÄØ¬µL of working solution to each tube.

  • Add 10‚ÄØ¬µL of your DNA sample (or Qubit standard) into each tube.

  • Vortex gently and incubate at room temperature for 2 minutes (light-sensitive!).

  • Insert tubes one at a time into the Qubit and record concentrations (ng/¬µL).

Expected results
  • 1‚Äì10‚ÄØng/¬µL: Ideal concentration range for downstream PCR
  • <1‚ÄØng/¬µL: May require re-extraction or increased PCR cycles
  • Controls:
    • Positive control ‚Üí should show DNA present
    • Negative control ‚Üí should read 0 ng/¬µL or ‚ÄúNot detected‚Äù
Coversion between ¬µL and mL
  • ¬µL ‚Üí mL: divide by 1,000 (e.g. 200 ¬µL √∑ 1,000 = 0.2 mL)
  • mL ‚Üí ¬µL: multiply by 1,000 (e.g. 1.5 mL √ó 1,000 = 1,500 ¬µL)

üìà PCR amplification

Goal: To amplify a target DNA barcode region from eDNA extracts to detectable levels using polymerase chain reaction (PCR).

  • PCR targets a barcode region used for species identification.

  • Primers are short, custom‚Äëmade DNA sequences that define the start and end points of the region to be amplified. They are ordered from suppliers such as IDT or Eurofins.

  • Two primers are needed:

    • Forward primer ‚Äì binds to the forward strand just before the barcode region, matching the sequence in the 5‚Ä≤ ‚Üí 3‚Ä≤ direction.

    • Reverse primer ‚Äì binds to the complementary strand just after the barcode region, but its sequence is still written in the 5‚Ä≤ ‚Üí 3‚Ä≤ direction by convention, even though it binds in the opposite orientation.

  • These primers flank the barcode, allowing DNA polymerase to copy the specific region between them, producing a copy or an amplicon containing the barcode.

Understanding primers and binding

DNA stores genetic instructions as a double‚Äëstranded helix made of nucleotides, each containing one of four bases.

Bases pair specifically (A with T, C with G), so if one strand reads:
5‚Ä≤‚Äì A T G C C A T ‚Äì3‚Ä≤
the complementary strand will read:
3‚Ä≤‚Äì T A C G G T A ‚Äì5‚Ä≤

DNA strands are antiparallel, running in opposite directions, with one end labeled 5‚Ä≤ (five prime) and the other 3‚Ä≤ (three prime). DNA synthesis always proceeds in the 5‚Ä≤ ‚Üí 3‚Ä≤ direction, which is important because DNA polymerases can only extend in this direction. This directionality must be considered when designing primers.

In PCR, two primers define the start and end of the region to be amplified.

Example: For a 1000‚ÄØbp DNA fragment with a barcode between positions 421 and 579:

Region Position range Example sequence
Forward primer 400‚Äì420 (5‚Ä≤ ‚Üí 3‚Ä≤) GTCGGTAAAACTCGTGCCAGC (MiFish-U-F)
Barcode region 421‚Äì579 AGCCTTGAGAACTGCTTAC...
Reverse primer 580‚Äì600 (5‚Ä≤ ‚Üí 3‚Ä≤ on reverse strand) CATAGTGGGGTATCTAATCCCAGTTTG (MiFish-U-R)

The primers flank the barcode, allowing DNA polymerase to copy the segment between them ‚Äî the amplicon ‚Äî which is then used for species identification.

  • PCR involves repeated thermal cycling:

    • Denaturation (~94‚Äì95‚ÄØ¬∞C): DNA strands are separated into single strands.

    • Annealing (~50‚Äì60‚ÄØ¬∞C): Forward and reverse primers bind to their complementary sequences flanking the target barcode.

    • Synthesis/Extension (72‚ÄØ¬∞C): DNA polymerase (e.g. Taq) synthesises new DNA strands between the primers.

  • This cycle is repeated ~35‚Äì40 times. Each cycle doubles the amount of target DNA.

The result is millions of identical amplicons of just the barcode region ‚Äî ready for sequencing.

  • Typical PCR mix includes:

    • eDNA extract
    • Forward & reverse primers
    • DNA polymerase (e.g. Taq)
    • dNTPs (A, T, C, G)
    • Buffer

  • Controls:

    • Positive control: contains known DNA to confirm amplification
    • Negative control: no DNA added ‚Äî detects contamination
    • Optimised annealing temperature: ensures specific primer binding

  • After PCR, amplicons can be verified using gel electrophoresis before sequencing.

What is Taq Polymerase and what does it do?

Taq polymerase is a DNA polymerase originally isolated from the heat-tolerant bacterium Thermus aquaticus.

  • It is heat-stable, meaning it remains active during high-temperature cycles (e.g. 94‚Äì95‚ÄØ¬∞C).
  • It is the most widely used enzyme for standard PCR because it:
    • Tolerates repeated heating and cooling
    • Efficiently synthesises DNA at ~72‚ÄØ¬∞C during the extension step

Taq polymerase builds a new strand of DNA by adding nucleotide building blocks (A, T, C, G) to a template strand.
It starts at the primer and matches each base with its complement:

  • A pairs with T
  • C pairs with G

This creates a new, complementary strand ‚Äî essentially copying the original DNA.

üß´ Gel electrophoresis

Goal: To visually confirm successful PCR amplification and screen for contamination.

  • PCR products are loaded into an agarose gel, a porous matrix made from a seaweed-derived polysaccharide.

  • When an electric current is applied:

    • Negatively charged DNA migrates toward the positive electrode
    • Smaller fragments move faster through the gel pores
    • Larger fragments move more slowly

  • After running the gel, DNA is stained with a fluorescent dye (e.g., GelRed or SYBR Safe) and visualised under UV or blue light.

  • Compare each sample band to a DNA ladder (size marker):

    • Clear band at expected size (e.g. 200‚Äì600 bp for 12S): PCR likely succeeded

      • bp (base pairs): the unit used to measure the length of a DNA fragment.
      • 1 bp = 1 base pair (A‚ÄìT or C‚ÄìG)
      • 200‚Äì600 bp means the DNA fragment is 200 to 600 base pairs long

    • Smearing or multiple bands: Non-specific amplification or degraded DNA

    • No band: PCR failed or DNA template was insufficient

  • Control lanes should show:

    • Positive control ‚Üí Band present at expected size
    • Negative control ‚Üí No bands (confirms absence of contamination)
    • Samples ‚Üí Bands at consistent sizes depending on target gene

üì¶ Sequencing

Goal: To determine which species are present in the sample by reading the DNA sequences of the amplified barcode regions (produced during PCR).

Steps:

  1. Pooling

    • PCR products (amplicons) from different samples are combined into a single sequencing library ‚Äî a collective pool of DNA fragments prepared for sequencing.

  2. Barcoding (Indexing)

    • Each sample is tagged with a unique short DNA sequence (barcode/index).
      ‚Üí This allows identification of which sequence came from which sample after sequencing.
      ‚Üí The process is known as multiplexing.

  3. Sequencing

    • The final library is loaded into a high-throughput sequencing platform, typically an Illumina MiSeq.

    • Illumina uses a method called sequencing by synthesis:

      • DNA fragments are copied one base at a time

      • As each base is added, it emits a fluorescent signal:

        • A = Adenine
        • T = Thymine
        • C = Cytosine
        • G = Guanine

      • A camera records these signals base-by-base as a sequence of coloured flashes

    • Each sequencing run produces FASTQ files containing:

      • Millions of short DNA reads (typically 150‚Äì300 base pairs)

      • Quality scores for each base

      • Each read ideally corresponds to a copy of the amplified barcode region.

    • Example: A read like AGCCTTGAGAACTGCTTAC... might be identified as European seabass through comparison with a reference database.

üíª Bioinformatics

Goal: To process and interpret raw DNA sequence data to identify which species were present in each sample.

  • After sequencing (e.g. on an Illumina MiSeq), each sample produces FASTQ files ‚Äî text files containing:

    • Millions of short DNA reads

    • A quality score for each base indicating confidence in base calls

  • Quality filtering:

    • Remove low-quality reads and unreliable base calls

    • Trim off:

      • Residual primers
      • Adapter sequences
      • Noisy bases at the ends
      • Common tools: cutadapt,fastp,Trimmomatic`

  • Denoising & dereplication:

    • This is where DADA2 plays a central role.
What is DADA2?

DADA2 (Divisive Amplicon Denoising Algorithm 2) is an R package that turns raw reads into exact sequences called Amplicon Sequence Variants (ASVs).

It models and corrects sequencing errors to recover the true biological sequences present in your samples.

Step What DADA2 Does
Learn error rates Builds a model of sequencing error patterns
Denoise Corrects errors and resolves true sequences
Dereplicate Merges identical reads into unique sequences + counts
Merge reads Combines paired-end reads (forward + reverse)
Remove chimeras Eliminates artificial recombination products from PCR
Assign taxonomy Labels ASVs using a reference database (e.g. MiFish, GenBank)

  • Taxonomic assignment:

    • ASVs are matched to known species or genera using a reference database.

    • Common databases:

      • GenBank ‚Äì Broad nucleotide database
      • MiFish ‚Äì Fish-specific 12S rRNA barcodes
      • BOLD ‚Äì Barcode of Life Data System (especially for COI gene)

    • Matching tools:

      • BLAST ‚Äì Finds the closest match in a database (e.g. ASV AGCCTTGAGAACTGCTTAC... ‚Üí Dicentrarchus labrax (European seabass), 99.3% identity)
      • QIIME2 ‚Äì End-to-end pipeline with taxonomy plugins (e.g. BLAST, naive Bayes)
      • MEGAN ‚Äì Converts BLAST results into taxonomic trees & summaries

  • This is where anonymous DNA fragments are assigned species names ‚Äî transforming data into ecological meaning.

Taxonomic assignment using BLAST and BASTA

BLAST stands for Basic Local Alignment Search Tool.

  • It compares your unknown DNA sequence (e.g. an ASV) against a reference database such as GenBank, BOLD, or MiFish to find the most similar known sequences.

  • It reports:

    • % Identity ‚Äì How similar your sequence is to the reference
    • Query Coverage ‚Äì How much of your ASV aligned with the reference
    • value ‚Äì The probability the match is random (lower is better)

  • Example:

    • ASV: AGCCTTGAGAACTGCTTAC‚Ä¶ Best match: Dicentrarchus labrax (European seabass)
    • Result: 99.3% identity, 98% coverage, E-value = 2e-50
    • Strong evidence that this ASV is from European seabass.

BASTA stands for BLAST-based Taxonomic Assignment.

  • BASTA is a tool that builds on BLAST results, providing automated and reproducible taxonomic assignment using the Last Common Ancestor (LCA) approach.

  • While BLAST gives a list of possible matches, BASTA summarises those hits and assigns the taxonomy based on:

    • A consensus of the top hits
    • User-defined thresholds for identity and coverage
    • Lowest common taxonomic rank shared by all top matches (LCA)
    • This helps avoid overly specific or incorrect species assignments.

BLAST finds best matching sequences in a database, and BASTA assigns taxonomy using consensus of BLAST hits.
